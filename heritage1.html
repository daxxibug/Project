Pompeii Archaeological Site

The Pompeii Archaeological Site in Campania, Italy, preserves the remains of the ancient Roman city destroyed by the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in AD 79. Buried under volcanic ash for centuries, the site offers an unparalleled snapshot of daily life in the Roman world and is a major component of the UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Key Facts:

  • Location: Pompei, Campania Italy
  • Area: ~66 ha (about 50 ha excavated)
  • UNESCO inscription: 1997 (criteria iii, iv, v)
  • Managing body: Pompeii Archaeological Park
  • Annual visitors: ≈4 million (2023)

Historic Background

Founded in the 7th–6th century BC, Pompeii developed from an Oscan and Samnite settlement into a prosperous Roman port and resort. The eruption of AD 79 buried the city under up to 6 m of ash and pumice. Eyewitness accounts by Pliny the Younger described the catastrophe that killed thousands and froze buildings, objects, and victims in time.

Leaning Tower of Pisa

The Leaning Tower of Pisa (Torre Pendente di Pisa) is a freestanding medieval bell tower in Pisa, Tuscany, Italy. Renowned worldwide for its unintended tilt, it stands beside the Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta in the Piazza dei Miracoli (Square of Miracles), a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Its distinctive lean and elegant Romanesque design make it one of the most recognizable architectural landmarks in the world

Key Facts:

  • Location: Piazza del Duomo, 56126 Pisa, Tuscany, Italy
  • Construction: Begun 1173, completed 1372
  • Height: 55.86-56.7 m (183-186 ft)
  • Current tilt: ~3.97° from vertical
  • Material: White marble and limestone

History and Construction

Construction began in 1173 as part of Pisa’s cathedral complex. Soft, unstable subsoil caused the foundation to sink soon after the first tiers were built, initiating the tilt. Work halted for almost a century—ironically allowing the ground to settle—before resuming under architects Giovanni di Simone and Tommaso di Andrea Pisano. Completed in 1372, the tower comprises eight stories, including a bell chamber housing seven bells tuned to the major scale.

Amalfi Coast

The Amalfi Coast is a celebrated stretch of Mediterranean coastline in southern Italy’s Campania region, extending along the southern edge of the Sorrentine Peninsula. Known for its dramatic cliffs, pastel-colored fishing villages, and terraced lemon groves, it has become one of the world’s most iconic coastal destinations and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Key Facts:

  • Location: Campania, Italy
  • Length: 1987
  • UNESCO inscription: 1997
  • Major Towns:Amalfi, Positano, Ravello, Praiano
  • Known for: Scenic drives, architecture, cuisine, and Mediterranean landscape

Geography and landscape

The Amalfi Coast runs from Vietri sul Mare to Positano, facing the Tyrrhenian Sea. Its steep terrain features limestone cliffs that plunge into the sea, interspersed with coves, beaches, and terraced gardens. The coastal highway, known as the Amalfitana (SS163), offers panoramic views but requires skilled driving through winding roads.
