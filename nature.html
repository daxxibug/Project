Gran Paradiso National Park

Gran Paradiso National Park (Italian: Parco Nazionale del Gran Paradiso) is a protected alpine area in the Aosta Valley and Piedmont regions of northwestern Italy. Established in 1922, it is Italy’s oldest national park and renowned for its dramatic mountain landscapes and conservation of the Alpine ibex.

Key Facts:

  • Location: Aosta Valley & Piedmont, Italy
  • Established: 1922
  • Area: ~703 km² (271 sq mi)
  • Highest Point: Gran Paradiso, 4,061 m (13,323 ft)
  • Notable Species: Alpine ibex, Chamois, Golden Eagle

History and Creation

The park originated as a royal hunting reserve for King Victor Emmanuel II, who protected the ibex population in the 19th century. In 1922, his grandson, King Victor Emmanuel III, donated the land to the Italian state, creating the first national park to promote wildlife preservation and scientific research.

Cinque Terre National Park

Cinque Terre National Park (Parco Nazionale delle Cinque Terre) is a protected coastal area in Liguria, northwestern Italy, known for its terraced cliffs, pastel-hued villages, and Mediterranean biodiversity. Established in 1999, it is Italy’s first national park and part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site “Portovenere, Cinque Terre, and the Islands.”

Key Facts:

  • Established: 1999
  • Area: 38.6 km² (3,868 ha)
  • Villages: Monterosso al Mare, Vernazza, Corniglia, Manarola, Riomaggiore
  • UNESCO status: Cultural Landscape since 1997
  • Governing Body: Ente Parco Nazionale delle Cinque Terre

Landscape and Ecology

The park covers a narrow, mountainous stretch of Ligurian coastline featuring steep terraces sustained by centuries-old dry-stone walls. Its biodiversity spans Mediterranean scrub, evergreen oak woods, and rocky marine ecosystems. Wildlife includes peregrine falcons, foxes, and seabirds, while protected marine zones preserve underwater habitats and coastal reefs.

Stelvio National Park

Stelvio National Park (Italian: Parco Nazionale dello Stelvio) is a protected alpine area spanning the regions of Lombardy and Trentino-Alto Adige in northern Italy. Established in 1935, it is one of Italy’s oldest and largest national parks, renowned for its glaciers, rugged peaks, and diverse alpine ecosystems.

Key Facts:

  • Established: 1935
  • Area: About 1,307 km²
  • Regions: Lombardy and Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol
  • Highest Peak: Ortler (3,905 m)
  • Governing Body: Parco Nazionale dello Stelvio Consortium

Geography and ecosystems

The park extends from 650 meters to nearly 4,000 meters in elevation, encompassing valleys, forests, glaciers, and alpine meadows. It borders Switzerland’s Swiss National Park and Italy’s Adamello Brenta Nature Park, forming part of one of Europe’s largest continuous protected mountain areas. Habitats range from coniferous forests to high-altitude tundra, supporting diverse flora and fauna.

Abruzzo, Lazio and Molise National Park

Abruzzo, Lazio and Molise National Park (Italian: Parco Nazionale d’Abruzzo, Lazio e Molise) is one of Italy’s oldest and most celebrated protected areas, established in 1923 in central Italy. It spans the Apennine Mountains across three regions—Abruzzo, Lazio, and Molise—and is renowned for its biodiversity, mountainous landscapes, and wildlife conservation.

Key Facts:

  • Established: 1923
  • Area: ~497 km² (192 mi²)
  • Regions: Abruzzo, Lazio, Molise
  • Highest Peak: Monte Petroso, 2,249 m
  • Main Species: Marsican brown bear, Apennine Wolf, Abruzzo Chamois

Geography and landscape

The park lies in the central Apennines, within the province of L’Aquila and parts of Frosinone and Isernia. Its terrain features rugged limestone mountains, forested valleys, karst plateaus, and high-altitude lakes such as Lake Barrea. Over two-thirds of the area is covered by beech forests, contributing to its recognition as part of a UNESCO World Heritage serial site for ancient beech woodlands.
