Basilica of St. Anthony

The Basilica of St. Anthony (Italian: Basilica di Sant’Antonio di Padova) is a major Roman Catholic church and pilgrimage site in Padua, Veneto, Italy. Dedicated to Saint Anthony of Padua, it is among the most visited shrines in the world and a landmark of medieval Christian art and devotion.

Key Facts:

  • Location: Padua, Veneto, Italy
  • Denomination: Roman Catholic (Minor Basilica)
  • Construction period: Begun 1232, completed mid-14th century
  • Architectural Style: Monte Calvo, Romanesque-Gothic with Byzantine and Moorish elements
  • Patron saint's tomb: Saint Anthony of Padua

History and Architecture

Construction of the basilica began shortly after St. Anthony’s death in 1231 to house his remains. The building combines elements from diverse traditions: Romanesque base structures, Gothic arches, and domes reminiscent of Byzantine influence. Its multiple domes and slender bell towers form a distinctive skyline visible across Padua, reflecting the city’s role as a crossroads of medieval culture and faith.

Monte Sant’Angelo Sanctuary

The Monte Sant’Angelo Sanctuary, or Sanctuary of Saint Michael the Archangel, is a UNESCO-listed pilgrimage site in Monte Sant’Angelo, Apulia, Italy. Built around a natural cave where the Archangel Michael is said to have appeared in 490 AD, it is among the oldest Christian sanctuaries in Western Europe and remains a center of devotion and art history.

Key Facts:

  • Location: Via Reale Basilica 127, Monte Sant’Angelo (FG), Italy
  • UNESCO status: “The Longobards in Italy: Places of Power” (2011)
  • Structure: Upper basilica, 86-step staircase, lower cave shrine
  • Founded: 5th century AD, Lombard reconstructions 7th century

Origins and legend

According to the Liber de apparitione sancti Michaelis, three apparitions of the Archangel between 490 and 493 AD sanctified the cave. The first involved a miraculous arrow that rebounded on a shepherd, the second brought victory over pagan enemies, and the third consecrated the grotto as Michael’s own church

Basilica of Santa Maria degli Angeli

The Basilica of Santa Maria degli Angeli is a monumental 16th-century papal church in the plain below Assisi, Umbria, Italy. Built to enshrine the tiny Porziuncola chapel—where Saint Francis founded the Franciscan Order—it remains one of the most important pilgrimage centers of Franciscan spirituality.

Key Facts:

  • Location: Piazza della Porziuncola 1, Santa Maria degli Angeli (Assisi)
  • Construction: 1565 – 1685; rebuilt 1836 – 1840 after earthquake
  • Architects: Galeazzo Alessi, Giacomo Giorgetti, later Luigi Poletti
  • Style: Late Renaissance and Baroque
  • Affiliation: Papal minor basilica, Franciscan Order

Historical background

Commissioned by Pope Pius V after the Council of Trent, the basilica was designed by the Perugian architect Galeazzo Alessi to honor the growing Franciscan movement and accommodate the many pilgrims drawn to the Porziuncola. The great earthquake of 1832 necessitated reconstruction under Luigi Poletti, and in 1925 – 1930 Cesare Bazzani redesigned the façade, adding the gilded bronze statue of the “Madonna degli Angeli” by Guglielmo Colasanti

Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore

The Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore, commonly known as the Florence Cathedral or the Duomo, is a masterpiece of Gothic and Renaissance architecture located in Piazza del Duomo, Florence, Italy. Its vast dome by Filippo Brunelleschi transformed Western architecture and remains the largest masonry dome ever constructed.

Key Facts:

  • Location: Piazza del Duomo, Florence, Italy
  • Construction: 1296–1436; façade completed 1887
  • Architects: Arnolfo di Cambio, Filippo Brunelleschi, Francesco Talenti, Emilio De Fabris
  • Height of Dome: 114.5 m (375 ft)
  • UNESCO status: Part of the Historic Centre of Florence

Origins and construction

Commissioned in 1296 to replace the earlier Santa Reparata church, the cathedral was designed by Arnolfo di Cambio as a monumental symbol of Florentine prosperity. Work proceeded under several architects, including Giotto and Francesco Talenti, before Filippo Brunelleschi engineered the revolutionary dome. Using a double-shell structure and a herringbone brick pattern, Brunelleschi completed the dome in 1436, solving one of the greatest technical challenges of the Renaissance.
