Portofino

Portofino is a small coastal village and harbor on the Italian Riviera in Liguria, Italy. Renowned for its colorful waterfront houses, luxury yachts, and natural harbor, it has long been a magnet for artists, celebrities, and travelers seeking the quintessential Mediterranean seaside charm.

Key Facts:

  • Region: Liguria, Italy
  • Population: Approximately 400 residents
  • Known for: Picturesque harbor, luxury tourism, and pastel-colored houses
  • Protected area: Part of the Portofino Regional Natural Parl
  • Nearest City: Genoa (about 36 km away)

Geography and setting

Located on a small peninsula in the Gulf of Tigullio, Portofino lies within the Metropolitan City of Genoa. Its natural harbor forms a semicircle lined with traditional fishing boats, high-end boutiques, and restaurants. The surrounding hills, covered with olive groves and Mediterranean pines, provide hiking trails and panoramic views over the Ligurian Sea.

Positano

Positano is a cliffside village on the Amalfi Coast in the Campania region of southern Italy. Known for its pastel-colored houses cascading toward the Tyrrhenian Sea, it is among Italy’s most photogenic and romantic coastal towns and a central highlight of the UNESCO World Heritage Site Amalfi Coast.

Key Facts:

  • Region: Campania, Province of Salerno
  • Population: ~3,800 residents
  • Elevation: 30 m above sea level
  • Famous landmark: Church of Santa Maria Assunta
  • UNESCO status: Part of the Amalfi Coast World Heritage Site

Geography and layout

Built vertically on the slopes of the Monti Lattari, Positano appears as a vertical maze of steps and narrow alleys connecting upper terraces to pebble beaches below. Its two principal beaches—Spiaggia Grande and Fornillo—offer contrasting atmospheres: one vibrant and social, the other quieter and favored by locals. Offshore lie the Li Galli islets, legendary home of the Sirens of Greek myth.

Sorrento

Sorrento is a picturesque coastal town in the Campania region of southern Italy, perched on cliffs overlooking the Bay of Naples. Renowned for its lemon groves, scenic vistas, and access to the Amalfi Coast, it blends Mediterranean charm with centuries of history. The town is a popular base for visiting Capri, Pompeii, and Mount Vesuvius.

Key Facts:

  • Region: Campania, Italy
  • Elevation: About 47 m (154 ft) above sea level
  • Population: ~16,000 (2024 est.)
  • Founded: Ancient Greek origins; developed under Roman rule
  • Famous for: Limoncello, marquetry art, sea views, and historic architecture

Geography and Setting

Sorrento lies about 50 km south of Naples on the Sorrentine Peninsula, facing Mount Vesuvius across the bay. The town’s clifftop terraces give panoramic views of the sea and nearby islands. Narrow lanes lined with citrus trees connect to small harbors such as Marina Grande and Marina Piccola, where ferries depart for Capri and Amalfi.
